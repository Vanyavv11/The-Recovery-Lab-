Initial Diagnosis

What I thought had been a sprain slowly turned into something I never imagined could have happened.

For the first few days after the injury, walking was difficult, but because I was still able to move around, I convinced myself I was fine. I limped through the airport, took a three-hour flight home, and planned to see a doctor only if it didn’t improve. Since the pain was manageable and I could still walk, I assumed it was getting better on its own.

Even so, I wasn’t normal. I still had a limp, stairs were difficult, and I couldn’t fully bend my knee. I kept noticing small improvements day to day, which made me delay getting it checked.

That changed when I tried to stand up from the floor and my knee suddenly gave out, bending farther than it had before. That was the first moment I seriously thought it might be more than a sprain.

After that, I started researching my symptoms—instability, swelling, pain, and the knee giving out. But I also focused on the fact that I had been able to ski, walk, and move right after the injury with relatively little pain.

That detail made everything more confusing. Most of what I found pointed toward a meniscus tear or sprain rather than a major ACL injury, so I continued to believe it wasn’t serious.

Then my knee gave out again at winter formal while I was dancing. I nearly fell, and the next day I went to urgent care.

At urgent care, an X-ray showed fluid in my knee but no fractures. I was referred to a sports medicine doctor for further evaluation.

During the exam, the doctor tested my knee’s stability and movement. I was unable to properly balance and twist on the injured leg. At the end of the visit, they said there was a real possibility of a partial or full ACL tear and scheduled an MRI.

Before the MRI results, I started physical therapy. My therapist initially thought it was unlikely to be a full ACL tear because I still had some function, could walk with a limp, and had decent mobility.

A few days later, I had the MRI. The scan itself was easy, but waiting for the results was not.

At my follow-up appointment, I was diagnosed with a full-thickness, grade 3 ACL tear. My ACL was completely ruptured.

I had expected many outcomes, but not this one. The doctor explained that surgery would be necessary if I wanted to return to sports safely, and that playing my upcoming badminton season would not be possible.

Hearing that made everything feel final. I had found out about the full extent of the injury weeks after it happened, and it still didn’t feel real.
