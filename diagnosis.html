Recovery Begins

The First Days After Injury

In the first few days after the injury, I could still walk with a limp, so I convinced myself it wasn’t serious. I flew home, tried to act normal, and told myself I would only get it checked if it didn’t improve.

Even though things weren’t normal, I kept focusing on small improvements. I still had limited motion, trouble with stairs, and a clear limp, but I kept telling myself it was getting better because I could still function.

The Moment Things Didn't Add Up

Everything changed when I tried to stand up from the floor and my knee suddenly gave out, bending farther than it had since the injury. That was the first moment I realized something deeper might be wrong.

After that, I searched symptoms, trying to convince myself it was a sprain or meniscus injury because I had been able to ski and walk after the fall. My knee giving out again at winter formal finally pushed me to seek medical care.

Urgent Care & Physical Therapy

An X-ray showed fluid in my knee but no fracture. A sports medicine physician raised the possibility of an ACL injury, but even then I struggled to believe it.

Before my MRI, my physical therapist thought a complete tear was unlikely. That gave me hope, but waiting for the MRI results became one of the hardest parts mentally.

The Diagnosis

At my follow-up appointment, I learned I had a full-thickness Grade 3 ACL tear. My ACL was completely ruptured.

My physical therapist was shocked. My doctor explained that surgery would be necessary to safely return to sports, and my upcoming badminton season was no longer possible.

“I learned that being able to move doesn't always mean nothing is seriously injured.”

What I Learned

The hardest part wasn't the MRI itself—it was living for weeks between hope and uncertainty. Looking back, that waiting period was just as mentally difficult as hearing the diagnosis.
