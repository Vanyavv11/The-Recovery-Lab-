Initial Diagnosis

What I thought had been a sprain slowly turned into something I never imagined could have happened.

The First Few Days

For the first few days after the injury, walking was difficult, but I was still able to move around. Because of that, I convinced myself it was fine. I limped through the airport, took a three-hour flight home, and planned to see a doctor only if it didn’t improve.

Even though I wasn’t normal, I noticed small day-to-day improvements. I still had a limp, stairs were difficult, and I couldn’t fully bend my knee, but it felt like it was slowly getting better.

The Knee Giving Out

Everything changed when I tried to stand up from the floor and my knee suddenly gave out, bending farther than it had before. That was the first moment I seriously thought something more serious might be wrong.

After that, I started researching my symptoms, including instability, swelling, and pain. I also focused on the fact that I had been able to ski, walk, and move right after the injury with relatively little pain.

That detail made everything more confusing, and I still convinced myself it was likely just a sprain or meniscus issue.

Then my knee gave out again at winter formal while dancing. I nearly fell, and the next day I went to urgent care.

Urgent Care & Initial Testing

At urgent care, an X-ray showed fluid in my knee but no fractures. I was referred to a sports medicine doctor for further evaluation.

During the exam, they tested my knee’s stability and movement. I was unable to properly balance and twist on the injured leg. The doctor said there was a possibility of a partial or full ACL tear and scheduled an MRI.

PT Before MRI

Before getting the MRI results, I started physical therapy. My therapist initially thought it was unlikely to be a full ACL tear because I still had some mobility, could walk with a limp, and had decent function.

A few days later, I had the MRI. The scan itself was easy, but waiting for the results was difficult.

The Diagnosis

At my follow-up appointment, I was diagnosed with a full-thickness, grade 3 ACL tear. My ACL was completely ruptured.

The doctor explained that surgery would be necessary if I wanted to return to sports safely, and that playing my upcoming badminton season would not be possible.

Hearing that made everything feel final. I had found out the full extent of the injury weeks after it happened, and it still didn’t feel real.
