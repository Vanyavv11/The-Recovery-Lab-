Pre-Hab: Preparing for Surgery

Theme: Restoring strength, range of motion, and confidence before ACL reconstruction.

Summary: Before surgery, I committed myself to physical therapy and daily rehabilitation to prepare my knee for ACL reconstruction. Those weeks taught me that recovery doesn't begin after surgery—it begins the moment you decide to take control of it.

My pre-hab journey began shortly after I learned that I had completely ruptured my ACL. After hearing the diagnosis and learning that surgery would be necessary, my focus shifted from wondering what was wrong with my knee to doing everything possible to prepare it for surgery. I quickly learned that the work done before ACL reconstruction can have a major impact on recovery afterward.

Starting Physical Therapy

I attended physical therapy twice a week, where each session began with an assessment of my knee followed by manual therapy to loosen my quad and calf muscles before moving into rehabilitation exercises. Most appointments lasted about an hour and focused on improving knee stability, restoring range of motion, and rebuilding strength.

The exercises included single-leg balance, mini squats, bridges, lunges as my knee became stronger, and daily work on achieving full knee extension. My physical therapist emphasized that restoring extension before surgery was one of the most important goals because it would make the recovery after surgery much smoother.

Going Beyond the Clinic

While physical therapy gave me the foundation, I quickly realized that two sessions each week wouldn't be enough on their own. I became fully committed to my rehabilitation outside of the clinic. Every day, I completed my home exercises, repeated movements from therapy, and carefully introduced additional exercises that challenged my balance, strength, and control without causing unnecessary pain.

As I became stronger, I also began incorporating movements that related more closely to sports. Exercises like heel taps from an elevated surface and lateral band walks helped improve my balance, stability, and side-to-side control. The more consistent I became, the more progress I noticed. My knee gradually became stronger, my movements became smoother, and my confidence steadily increased.

Seeing Progress

One of the biggest milestones during pre-hab was regaining full knee extension. That single improvement made walking feel much more natural and gave me confidence that my hard work was paying off. My quad activation also improved significantly, allowing me to progress into more challenging strengthening exercises while continuing to improve my overall knee function.

The improvements surprised not only me, but also my parents, coaches, and physical therapist. Looking back, I realized that recovery wasn't about making one huge leap forward. It was the result of showing up every day and putting in consistent effort, even when the progress seemed small.

Why Pre-Hab Matters

Throughout this process, my doctors explained that pre-habilitation plays an important role in recovery after ACL reconstruction. Regaining range of motion, reducing swelling, restoring quad activation, and strengthening the muscles surrounding the knee before surgery can significantly improve the rehabilitation process afterward.

At the time, I viewed pre-hab as something I simply had to complete before surgery. Looking back, I now understand that it became the foundation for everything that followed during my recovery.

Reflection

The biggest lesson I learned during pre-hab was that consistency creates progress. It wasn't one difficult workout or one physical therapy appointment that improved my knee. It was the commitment to keep showing up every single day, even when I was tired, frustrated, or didn't feel like I was improving.

Pre-hab gave me more than a stronger knee. It taught me discipline, patience, and the importance of taking an active role in my own recovery. Looking back, I believe the work I put in before surgery became one of the biggest reasons my post-operative recovery started as successfully as it did.
