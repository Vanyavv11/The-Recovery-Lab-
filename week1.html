ACL Recovery Journal · Week 1

The Beginning of Recovery

What started as a focus on reducing swelling and regaining mobility quickly became a much more difficult process than expected, with significant pain, stiffness, and muscle shutdown during the earliest stage of rehabilitation.

The First Days

In the first few days after surgery, I was not able to do any real exercises at all. There was essentially zero quad activation, and my entire leg felt like dead weight due to how much muscle had shut down.

A large part of my time was spent dealing with pain, pressure, and stiffness in the knee. I was barely able to walk, and even standing up often caused sharp, shooting pain along with a strong pressure sensation.

My doctor explained that this was likely related to fluid shifting around inside the joint after surgery.

Week 1 Focus

Managing pain and swelling, protecting the reconstructed knee, restoring basic movement, and beginning to reconnect with a completely inactive quad.

First steps after surgery: Walking with the brace and crutches while the nerve block was still active. The knee felt extremely stiff, and moving independently was difficult.

Early Post-Op Experience

Learning to Move Again

Even basic movement required a lot of effort. The brace kept the knee protected, while the crutches helped support nearly all of my weight.

Because the quad was not activating, lifting or controlling the leg was extremely difficult. Most movement depended on using my arms, the brace, and help from someone else.

Nerve Block & Pain Onset

When the Pain Started

I had a nerve block on day 1, which meant I did not feel much immediately after surgery. However, once it wore off in the middle of the night, the pain became very intense, and that was when the real recovery process began.

First time seeing the incisions: The bandages had been removed and the area had been cleaned. Several healing incision sites were visible, with small stitches and adhesive still present.

One-Week Post-Op Appointment

Seeing the Knee for the First Time

At my one-week post-op appointment, my doctor removed the bandages and steri-strips. Seeing my knee for the first time was overwhelming because there were two larger incisions and three smaller ones.

I had chosen a quadriceps tendon graft, and it was clear how much had been done inside the knee. At this stage, the quad muscle had essentially shut down, making the leg feel completely like dead weight.

Medication & Pain Management

Controlling Pain and Pressure

I was taking a prescribed medication called Vicodin, but it did not significantly reduce the pain even when taking two doses. It did make me very sleepy around four to five hours after taking it, which is why I often relied on it at night.

Because the pain and pressure were still strong, my doctor prescribed a stronger five-day steroid, which I took daily. This noticeably helped reduce swelling and pressure over the following days.

Early Recovery Plan

Preparing to Start Physical Therapy

They also recommended that I begin physical therapy as soon as possible to start restoring movement and muscle activation.

Early rehabilitation was focused on managing swelling, regaining basic movement, and slowly beginning to re-engage the quad as pain allowed.

The End of Week 1

Week 1 was centered on pain control, protection, swelling management, and learning how to move with a leg that still had almost no muscle control. Progress was limited, but the foundation for rehabilitation had begun.
